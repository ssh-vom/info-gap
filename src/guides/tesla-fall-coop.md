## The thing nobody told me

I didn't know what Leetcode was until third year. By then, half the people I was
competing with had been grinding it since first year. This is the gap I'm talking
about — not talent, *timing and information*.

Here's the timeline that actually worked, and what I'd do sooner if I could.

## Timeline

- **Jan of 2nd year:** start Leetcode. Just "easy" problems, 30 min a day.
- **May of 2nd year:** first co-op at a no-name company. Worth it for the resume line, not the work.
- **Sep of 3rd year:** mediums, system design basics, and a real project on GitHub.
- **Jan of 3rd year:** applications open for fall. Apply to *everything* in the first 48 hours.

The last point is the one that mattered most. Referrals and early applications
beat a polished application submitted on day 14.

## What I'd skip

The "grind 500 problems" advice. After ~80 well-chosen problems covering the
core patterns (sliding window, BFS/DFS, two pointers, DP basics), you hit
diminishing returns. Spend that time on one project you can actually talk about
in an interview.

> A project you can defend in depth beats ten projects on your resume.

## The interview itself

Tesla's loop for me was: recruiter screen → technical (1h, 2 mediums) →
behavioral + 1 systems chat → onsite with 3 engineers.

The behavioral round is where people lose it. They want to hear *specific
decisions* and *tradeoffs you considered*, not "I'm passionate." Have 3 stories
ready, each with a concrete number and a concrete mistake you made and fixed.
